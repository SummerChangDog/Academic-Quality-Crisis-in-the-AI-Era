###### Abstract.
We provide a closed formula for the graded Betti numbers in the linear strands of all powers of binomial edge ideals
$J_{G}$
arising from closed graphs
$G$
that do not have the complete graph
$K_{4}$
as an induced subgraph. We show that these agree with the corresponding Betti numbers for the powers of the lexicographic initial ideal of
$J_{G}$
, thereby confirming a conjecture of Ene–Rinaldo–Terai in a special case.
## Introduction
Binomial edge ideals, introduced by Herzog, Hibi, Hreinsdóttir, Kahle, and Rauh in
[
7
]
and Ohtani in
[
9
]
, are ideals generated by binomials corresponding to the edges of a simple graph. Specifically, the binomial edge ideal of a graph
$G$
with edge set
$E(G)$
is
$$J_{G}=\left(x_{i}y_{j}-y_{i}x_{j}:\{i,j\}\in E(G)\right).$$
These ideals have been extensively studied due to their rich algebraic structure and deep connections to combinatorial properties of the underlying graphs; see
[
5
, Chapter 7]
.
In particular, the property of a labeled graph of being closed has proven fundamental in the study of binomial edge ideals. This property ensures that the minimal generators of
$J_{G}$
form a Gröbner basis with respect to the lexicographic order.
We denote by
$\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}$
the initial ideal with respect to this order.
A conjecture by Ene, Rinaldo and Terai in
[
4
, Conjecture 6.2]
asserts that for a closed graph
${G}$
the graded Betti numbers of
$J_{G}^{m}$
and
$\operatorname{in}_{lex}{J_{G}^{m}}$
are equal for all
$m\geq 1$
. In the case of the first power (
$m=1$
) this conjecture was recently proved by Peeva in
[
10
]
. Prior work by Herzog–Kiani–Madani in
[
6
]
and by Almousa–VandeBogert in
[
2
]
had determined the linear strands in the resolutions of
$J_{G}$
and
$\operatorname{in}_{lex}{J_{G}}$
for closed graphs
$G$
, showing in particular that the Betti numbers in these strands were equal. To our knowledge the linear strands of powers of binomial edge ideals have not yet been determined in the literature.
In this paper, we make progress towards the Ene-Rinaldo-Terai conjecture by providing a closed formula for the graded Betti numbers in the linear strands of all powers
$J_{G}^{m}$
and
$\operatorname{in}_{lex}{J_{G}^{m}}$
when
$G$
is a closed graph that does not admit the complete graph on four vertices as an induced subgraph. In particular, under these hypotheses the Betti numbers in the linear strand only depend on the number of edges and triangles of
$G$
.
Our main result is the following.
###### ( 3.6 ) .
Let
$G$
be closed and
$K_{4}$
-free graph with
$e$
edges and
$t$
triangles. Then for each
$m\geq 1$
and
$i\geq 0$
the following identities hold
$$\beta_{i,2m+i}(J_{G}^{m})=\beta_{i,2m+i}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}J_{G}^{m})=\binom{e+m-i-1}{m-i}\binom{2t}{i}.$$
Our methods employ tools from the study of Rees algebras of determinantal facet ideals. In particular, crucial to our work are the results of Almousa–Lin–Liske in
[
1
]
, summarized here in
2.1
.
## Background
A graph
$G$
is
closed
if there exists a labeling of the vertices such that for all edges
$\{i,j\}$
and
$\{k,l\}$
with
$i<j,\ k<l$
, we dhave that
$\{j,l\}\in E(G)$
if
$i=k$
and
$\{i,k\}\in E(G)$
if
$j=l$
. However, there are many equivalent definitions; see
[
5
, section 7.1.1]
. A clique
$\Delta$
of
$G$
is an induced complete subgraph of
$G$
. We denote the
$3$
-cycle graph by
$C_{3}$
and the complete graph on
$4$
vertices by
$K_{4}$
.
The
lexicographic order
on the polynomial ring
$R=\mathbb{K}[x_{1},\ldots,x_{n}]$
with coefficients in a field
$\mathbb{K}$
induced by
$x_{1}>x_{2}>\cdots>x_{n}$
is the dictionary order where
$x_{1}^{a_{1}}\cdots x_{n}^{a_{n}}<_{lex}x_{1}^{b_{1}}\cdots x_{n}^{b_{n}}$
provided the leftmost nonzero entry of
$(a_{1}-b_{1},\ldots,a_{n}-b_{n})$
is negative.
For an ideal
$I\subset R$
let
$\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(I)$
denote the ideal generated by the leading monomials of elements of
$I$
with respect to the lexicographic ordering.
A graded free resolution is a sequence of free
$R$
-modules
$F_{i}$
and degree-preserving
$R$
-module homomorphisms
$d_{i}$
$$0\rightarrow F_{p}\xlongrightarrow{d_{p}}\cdots\xlongrightarrow{d_{3}}F_{2}\xlongrightarrow{d_{2}}F_{1}\xlongrightarrow{d_{1}}F_{0}$$
(2.1)
such that
${\rm Ker}(d_{i})={\rm Im}(d_{i+1})$
for each
$i$
with
$1\leq i\leq p$
. Sequence (
2.1
) is termed a free resolution for the
$R$
-module
$M=\operatorname{Coker}(d_{1})$
.
A free resolution is minimal if
$\operatorname{Im}(d_{i})\subseteq\mathfrak{m}F_{i-1}$
for each
$i$
, where
$\mathfrak{m}$
denotes the homogeneous maximal ideal of
$R$
.
Let
$R(-j)$
denote the ring
$R$
with grading shifted according to the rule
$[R(-j)]_{i}=R_{i-j}$
.
The
graded Betti number
$\beta_{ij}(M)$
is the number of times
$R(-j)$
appears as a summand in the
$i$
-th free module of a minimal free resolution of
$M$
. More formally, with notation as in (
2.1
), we have
$$F_{i}=\bigoplus_{j}R(-j)^{\beta_{ij}}\text{ for all }i,j.$$
The
$i$
-th syzygy module of
$M$
is the
$R$
-module
$\Omega^{i}(M)=\ker(d_{i})$
, uniquely determined up to isomorphism. If
$M$
is generated in degree
$d$
, we let
$F_{i}^{\rm lin}$
be the direct summand
$R(-d-i)^{\beta_{i,d+i}}$
of
$F_{i}$
. Since
$d_{i}(F_{i}^{\rm lin})\subseteq F_{i-1}^{\rm lin}$
,
the complex obtained by restricting the differential of (
2.1
) to the free modules
$F_{i}^{\rm lin}$
is a subcomplex of (
2.1
), which
we call the
linear strand
of the resolution of
$M$
. It has the form
$$0\rightarrow R(-d-p)^{\beta_{p,d+p}}\xlongrightarrow{d_{p}}\cdots\xlongrightarrow{d_{3}}R(-d-2)^{\beta_{2,d+2}}\xlongrightarrow{d_{2}}R(-d-1)^{\beta_{1,d+1}}\xlongrightarrow{d_{1}}R(-d)^{\beta_{0d}}.$$
Let
$G$
be a graph with vertex set
$V=[n]$
and binomial edge ideal
$J_{G}$
. Set
$$f_{ij}=x_{i}y_{j}-x_{j}y_{i}\text{ and }J_{G}=(\ f_{ij}:\{i,j\}\in E(G)).$$
(2.2)
The
Rees algebra
of
$J_{G}$
is the graded
$R$
-algebra
$$\mathcal{R}(J_{G}):=\bigoplus_{i\geq 0}J_{G}^{i}T^{i}\subset R[T].$$
It admits a surjective ring homomorphism
$$\varphi:R[T_{ij}\mid(i,j)\in E(G)]\longrightarrow\mathcal{R}(J_{G}),\quad r\mapsto r,\text{ for }r\in R,\quad T_{ij}\mapsto f_{ij}T.$$
(2.3)
The kernel of
$\varphi$
corresponds to the relations of the Rees algebra of
$J_{G}$
. The Rees algebra is bigraded, with
$\deg(T)=(1,0)$
,
$\deg(x_{i})=\deg(y_{i})=(0,1)$
and the map
$\varphi$
is degree-preserving, which implies
$\deg(T_{ij})=(1,2)$
.
The Rees algebra of the initial ideal is defined analogously:
$\mathcal{R}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G})=\bigoplus_{i\geq 0}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}J_{G})^{i}T^{i}\subset R[T]$
. It is shown in
[
4
, Equation (3)]
that for a closed graph
$G$
identities
$\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G}^{i})=(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}J_{G})^{i}$
hold for all
$i\geq 1$
. Therefore, we have that
$\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(\mathcal{R}(J_{G}))=\mathcal{R}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}J_{G})$
.
When
$G$
is a closed graph, Almousa-Liske–Lin showed that the relations of the Rees algebra of its binomial edge ideal and initial ideal can be described explicitly as follows.
###### ( [ 1 , Theorem 4.5] ) .
Let
$G$
be a closed graph with cliques
$\Delta_{1},\ldots,\Delta_{s}$
. For
$\{i,j\}\in E(G)$
write
$\{i,j\}\in\Delta_{a}$
if
$a$
is the smallest index of a cliques that contains
$\{i,j\}$
. Adopt the notation in (
2.2
) and (
2.3
). In particular let
$T_{ij}$
be such that
$\varphi(T_{ij})=f_{ij}$
and
$\varphi(T_{ij})=\operatorname{in}_{lex}(f_{ij})$
, respectively.
The relations of
$\mathcal{R}(J_{G})$
are generated by:
(1)
$${x_{i}y_{j}T_{i^{\prime}j^{\prime}}}-x_{j}y_{i}T_{i^{\prime}j^{\prime}}-x_{i^{\prime}}y_{j^{\prime}}T_{ij}+x_{j^{\prime}}y_{i^{\prime}}T_{ij}\text{ for }\{i,j\}\in\Delta_{a},\{i^{\prime},j^{\prime}\}\in\Delta_{b}$$
with
$a\neq b$
(Koszul relations);
(2)
$${x_{i}T_{jk}}-x_{j}T_{ik}+x_{k}T_{ij}\text{ and }{y_{j}T_{ik}}-y_{i}T_{jk}-y_{k}T_{ij}$$
(2.4)
for
$\{i,j,k\}$
an induced
$C_{3}$
in
$G$
with
$i<j<k$
(Eagon-Northcott relations);
(3)
$${T_{ij}T_{kl}}-T_{ik}T_{jl}+T_{il}T_{jk}=0$$
where
$1\leq i<j<k<l\leq n$
and
$\{i,j,k,l\}$
induce a
$K_{4}$
subgraph of
$G$
(Plücker relations).
The relations of
$\mathcal{R}(\operatorname{in}_{lex}(J_{G}))$
are generated by:
(1)
$${x_{i}y_{j}T_{i^{\prime}j^{\prime}}}-x_{i^{\prime}}y_{j^{\prime}}T_{ij}$$
for
$i>i^{\prime},j>j^{\prime}$
,
$\{i,j\}\in\Delta_{a}$
,
$\{i^{\prime},j^{\prime}\}\in\Delta_{b}$
with
$a<b$
(Koszul type relations);
(2)
$\displaystyle{x_{i}T_{jk}}-x_{j}T_{ik}\text{ and }{y_{j}T_{ik}}-y_{k}T_{ij}$
(2.5)
for
$\{i<j<k\}$
an induced
$C_{3}$
in
$G$
(Eagon-Northcott type relations);
(3)
$${T_{ij}T_{j^{\prime}i^{\prime}}}-T_{\min}T_{\max}$$
for
$i\leq j$
and
$i^{\prime}\geq j^{\prime}$
,
$\min=(\min\{i,i^{\prime}\},\min\{j,j^{\prime}\})$
,
$\max=(\max\{i,i^{\prime}\},\max\{j,j^{\prime}\})$
(Plücker type relations).
In the rest of the paper we will focus on the case when the Plücker and Plücker type relations given in
2.1
are absent. In this case the ideal
$J_{G}$
is said to be of linear type.
Also useful to our analysis are two monomial orders on
$S=R[T_{ij}:(i,j)\in E(G)]$
. When analyzing relations of
$\mathcal{R}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}(J_{G})})$
we will use the reverse lexicographic order on
$S$
induced by the following ordering of the variables
$\displaystyle T_{ij}>T_{k\ell}\text{ if and only if }i<j\text{ or }i=j\text{ and }k<\ell$
(2.6)
$\displaystyle T_{ij}>x_{a},y_{b}\text{ for all }i,j,a,b$
$\displaystyle x_{1}>x_{2}>\cdots x_{n}>y_{1}>y_{2}>\cdots>y_{n}.$
It is proven in
[
1
, Theorem 3.14]
that there is a
different
monomial ordering
$\prec$
on
$S$
so that the leading monomials of the relations of
$\mathcal{R}(J_{G})$
are the same as those of the corresponding relations for
$\mathcal{R}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G}))$
.
Thus we always use
$\prec$
when analyzing relations of
$\mathcal{R}(J_{G})$
.

### Syzygies via relations of the Rees algebra
In this section, we consider a more general setup than in the rest of the paper. Later, we will apply these results to the binomial edge ideals of closed graphs and to their initial binomial edge ideals.
Let
$R$
be a grade
$\mathbb{K}$
-algebra and let
$I=(f_{1},\ldots,f_{s})$
be a homogeneous ideal
of
$R$
. Consider the
$R$
-algebra map
$\varphi:R[T_{1},\ldots,T_{s}]\to\mathcal{R}(I)$
given by
$\varphi(r)=r$
for
$r\in r$
and
$\varphi(T_{i})=f_{i}$
. We view
$R[T_{1},\ldots,T_{s}]$
as a bigraded ring with
$\deg(r)=(0,\deg_{R}(r))$
and
$\deg(T_{i})=(1,\deg(f_{i}))$
.
We denote
$L=\ker(\varphi)$
and define the following subsets of
$L$
$\displaystyle L_{(a,b)}$
$\displaystyle=$
$\displaystyle\{F\in L:\deg(F)=(a,b)\},$
$\displaystyle L_{(a,\geq b)}$
$\displaystyle=$
$\displaystyle\{F\in L:\deg(F)=(a,c)\text{ for some }c\geq b\},$
$\displaystyle L_{(a,-)}$
$\displaystyle=$
$\displaystyle L_{(a,\geq 0)}.$
Observe that the first set is a
$\mathbb{K}$
-vector space and the last two are
$R-$
modules.
To give a more explicit description of these sets we utilize the notation
$|\alpha|=\sum_{i=1}^{s}\alpha_{i}$
for a vector
$\alpha=(\alpha_{1},\ldots,\alpha_{s})\in\mathbb{Z}^{s}$
.
We have
$\displaystyle L_{(m,-)}$
$\displaystyle=$
$\displaystyle\{F\in R[T_{1},\ldots,T_{s}]:\varphi(F)=0,\deg{F}=(m,-)\}$
(3.1)
$\displaystyle=$
$\displaystyle\left\{F=\sum_{\alpha\in\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^{s},|\alpha|=m}r_{\alpha}T_{i}^{\alpha_{i}}\in R[T_{1},\ldots,T_{s}]:\sum_{\alpha\in\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^{s},|\alpha|=m}r_{\alpha}f_{i}^{\alpha_{i}}=0\right\}.$
We will approach the syzygies of powers of
$I$
via the Rees algebra by means of the following lemma.
###### .
Let
$R$
be a graded
$\mathbb{K}$
-algebra with
$R_{0}=\mathbb{K}$

$I$

$\{f_{1},\dots,f_{s}\}$

Then there is an isomorphism between

$N=L_{(m,-)}/L_{(m,\geq md)}$

###### Proof.
Let
$\{h_{1},...,h_{t}\}\subset\{f^{\alpha}:|\alpha|=m\}$
be a minimal generating set of
$I^{m}$
as a
$\mathbb{K}$
-vector space. Considering a basis
$e_{1},\ldots,e_{t}$
for the free module
$R^{t}$
and elements
$r_{1},\ldots,r_{t}\in R$
one has an
$R$

$$\partial:R^{t}\rightarrow R,\quad\partial\left(\sum_{i=1}^{t}r_{i}e_{i}\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{t}r_{i}h_{i}.$$
Recall that
$\Omega_{1}(I^{m})=\ker(\partial)$
.
For each
$1\leq i\leq t$

$\alpha^{(i)}=(\alpha^{(i)}_{1},\ldots,\alpha^{(i)}_{s})\in\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^{s}$
such that the minimal generator

Consider the following map
$$g_{0}:\Omega_{1}(I^{m})\rightarrow R[T],\quad g_{0}\left(\sum_{i=1}^{t}r_{i}e_{i}\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{t}r_{i}T_{1}^{\alpha_{1}^{(i)}}\cdots T_{s}^{\alpha_{s}^{(i)}}.$$
(3.2)

$$\varphi\left(\sum_{i=1}^{t}r_{i}T_{1}^{\alpha_{1}^{(i)}}\cdots T_{s}^{\alpha_{s}^{(i)}}\right)=T^{m}\sum_{i=1}^{t}r_{i}h_{i}=T^{m}\partial\left(\sum_{i=1}^{t}r_{i}e_{i}\right)=0.$$
Thus, the image of

The map

Recall from (

.
Then equation (
3.3
) becomes
$$\sum_{\,|\alpha^{(i)}\,|=m}r_{i}T^{\alpha^{(i)}}\in L_{(m,-)}\iff\sum_{\,|\alpha^{(i)}\,|=m}r_{i}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{t}a_{ij}h_{j}\right)=0.$$
(3.5)
Using notation introduced in (
3.4
), we define
$$p_{0}:L_{(m,-)}\rightarrow\Omega_{1}\left(I^{m}\right),\quad p_{0}\left(\sum_{|\alpha^{(i)}|=m}r_{i}T_{1}^{\alpha_{1}^{(i)}}\cdots T_{s}^{\alpha_{s}^{(i)}}\right)=\sum_{\,|\alpha^{(i)}\,|=m}r_{i}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{t}a_{ij}e_{i}\right).$$
(3.6)
By (
3.5
) it follows that
$\displaystyle\partial\left(\sum_{\,|\alpha^{(i)}\,|=m}r_{i}\sum_{j=1}^{t}a_{ij}e_{j}\right)=$
$\displaystyle\sum_{\,|\alpha^{(i)}\,|=m}r_{i}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{t}a_{ij}h_{j}\right)=0.$
Thus the image of
$p_{0}$
is contained in the syzygy module
$\Omega^{1}(I^{m})$
.
We now wish to show that
$p_{0}:L_{(m,-)}\to\Omega_{1}(I^{m})$
induces a well defined map
$\overline{p_{0}}:N\to\Omega_{1}(I^{m})$
.
It is enough to show that
$L_{(m,md)}$
is contained in the kernel of
$p_{0}$
. For
$F=\sum_{|\alpha^{(i)}|=m}c_{i}T_{1}^{\alpha_{1}^{(i)}}\cdots T_{s}^{\alpha_{s}^{(i)}}$
we see that
$\deg{(F)}=(m,md)$
if and only if
$\deg_{R}(c_{i})=0$
if and only if
$c_{i}\in\mathbb{K}$
.
Then setting
$b_{j}=\sum_{i}c_{i}a_{ij}\in\mathbb{K}$
yields
$$p_{0}(F)=p_{0}\left(\sum_{i}c_{i}T_{1}^{\alpha_{1}^{(i)}}\cdots T_{s}^{\alpha_{s}^{(i)}}\right)=\sum_{i}c_{i}\left(\sum_{j}a_{ij}e_{j}\right)=\sum_{j=1}^{t}b_{j}e_{j}.$$
If
$F\in L_{(m,mn)}$
then
$p_{0}(F)=\sum_{j=1}^{t}b_{j}e_{j}\in\Omega_{1}(I^{m})$
, thus we have
$\partial\left(\sum_{j=1}^{t}b_{j}e_{j}\right)=\sum_{j=1}^{t}b_{j}h_{j}=0$
.
It follows by linear independence of
$h_{1},\ldots,h_{s}$
over
$\mathbb{K}$
that
$b_{j}=0$
for all
$1\leq j\leq t$
. Therefore,
$$p_{0}(F)=\sum_{j=1}^{t}b_{j}e_{j}=0.$$
We have shown that
$L_{(m,md)}$
is contained in the kernel of
$p_{0}$
, thus there is a well-defined
$R$
-module homomorphism
$\overline{p_{0}}:N\to\Omega_{1}(I^{m})$
.
Lastly one can check that
$p_{0}$
and
$g_{1}$
are mutually inverse maps using equations (
3.2
) and (
3.6
), and the fact that
$g_{1}$
is induced by
$g_{0}$
.
∎
While the previous result concerns only the first syzygy modules of the powers of an ideal, under additional assumptions on the relations of the Rees algebra we can use the Koszul complex to find a formula for the entire linear strand of the powers.
###### .
Let
$I$
be a homogeneous ideal with minimal homogeneous generating set
$\{f_{1},\dots,f_{s}\}$
of elements of degree
$d$
. Let
$S=R[T_{1},\ldots,T_{s}]$
and let
$L\subset S$
denote the ideal of relations of the Rees algebra
$\mathcal{R}(I)$
.
If
$L_{(1,d+1)}$
is spanned by a
$S$
-regular sequence
$g_{1},\dots,g_{r}$
and satisfies
$L_{(m,md+1)}=(g_{1},\dots,g_{r})_{(m,md+1)}$
and
$L_{(m,md)}=0$
for some
$m\geq 1$
, then the Betti numbers in the linear strand of
$I^{m}$
are given by
$$\beta_{(i,md+i)}(I^{m})=\binom{r}{i}\binom{s+m-i-1}{m-i}.$$
###### Proof.
Set
$E=(g_{1},\ldots,g_{r}s)\subset S$
and consider the Koszul complex
$K_{\bullet}$
of the regular sequence
$g_{1},\dots,g_{r}$
. This is a minimal free resolution for
$S/E$
:
$$K_{\bullet}:0\to S(-m,-m(d+1))^{r\choose m}\to\ldots\to S(-i,-i(d+1))^{r\choose i}\to\ldots\to S(-1,-(d+1))^{r\choose 1}\to S\to 0.$$
Restricting bidegrees to
$(m,\geq md)$
,
and augmenting by
$S(m,\geq md)\to I^{m}\to 0$
on the right, we obtain the complex
$$0\to S(-m,-m(d+1))^{r\choose m}_{(m,\geq md)}\to\ldots\to S(-i,-i(d+1))_{(m,\geq md)}^{r\choose i}\to\ldots\to S_{(m,\geq md)}\to I^{m}\to 0.$$
(3.7)
Observe that in each bidegree
$(u,v)$
there is a graded
$\mathbb{K}$
-vector space isomorphism
$$S_{(u,v)}=\bigoplus_{1\leq i_{1}\leq i_{2}\leq\cdots\leq i_{u}\leq s}R_{v-du}\prod_{j=1}^{u}T_{i_{j}}\cong{R_{v-du}}^{\binom{s+u-1}{u}}.$$
(3.8)
This yields that
$S(-i,-i(d+1))_{(m,\geq md)}$
is a free-graded
$R$
-module with the following decomposition:
$$\begin{split}S(-i,-i(d+1))_{(m,\geq md)}=S_{(m-i,\geq md-i(d+1))}=S_{(m-i,\geq d(m-i)}(0,-i)\\
\stackrel{{\scriptstyle\eqref{eq: free1}}}{{\cong}}\left[\bigoplus_{v\geq d(m-i)}{R_{v-d(m-i)}}^{\binom{s+(m-i)-1}{m-i}}\right](-i)=R(-i)^{\binom{s+(m-i)-1}{m-i}}.\end{split}$$
(3.9)
Thus (
3.7
) is an augmented linear complex of free
$R$
-modules.
Note that, while it is exact in higher homological degrees, the complex (
3.7
) is not necessarily exact at
$S(-1,-n)_{(m,\geq md)}^{r\choose 1}\to S_{(m,\geq md)}\to I^{m}$
since we have
$\ker(S_{(m,\geq md)}\to I^{m})=L$
, not
$E$
.
However, restricting the sequence displayed above to bidegree
$(m,md+1)$
we have
$\displaystyle\ker(S_{(m,md+1)}\to[I^{m}]_{md+1})=L_{(m,md+1)}$
(3.10)
$\displaystyle\operatorname{Im}(S(-1,-d)_{(m,md+1)}^{\binom{r}{1}}\to S_{(m,md+1)})=E_{(m,md+1)}.$
(3.11)
Equality in (
3.10
) follows from
3.2
and the hypothesis
$L_{(m,md)}=0$
.
The vector spaces in (
3.10
) and (
3.11
) are equal
by the hypothesis
$L_{(m,md+1)}=(g_{1},\dots,g_{r})_{(m,md+1)}=E_{(m,md+1)}$
. Since the complex (
3.7
) is exact in degrees
$md$
and
$md+1$
, it is isomorphic to the linear strand in the
$R$
-free resolution of
$I^{m}$
by
[
6
, Corollary 1.2]
.
Thus we obtain the formula
$\beta_{i,md+i}(I^{m})={r\choose i}{s+m-i-1\choose m-i}$
by combining (
3.7
) and (
3.9
).
∎
### The linear strand of $J_{G}^{m}$
If
$G$
is a closed graph that is
$K_{4}$
-free, then the Rees algebra of
$J_{G}$
does not exhibit any Plücker relations and the Rees algebra of
$\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G})$
does not have any Plücker-type relations; see
2.1
for the definition of these relations which shows that they correspond to
$K_{4}$
subgraphs. Therefore we can derive from
3.2
a particularly simple expression for the first syzygies of powers of
$J_{G}$
.
###### .
Let
$G$
be a closed graph with no induced
$K_{4}$
. Let
$L$
denote the ideal of relations of the Rees algebra
$\mathcal{R}(J_{G})$
and let
$L^{\prime}$
the ideal of relations of the Rees algebra
$\mathcal{R}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}J_{G})$
. Then, with notation as in (
3.1
), for each integer
$m\geq 1$
there are graded
$R$
-module isomorphisms
$$L_{(m,-)}\cong\Omega^{1}(J_{G}^{m})\qquad\text{and}\qquad L^{\prime}_{(m,-)}\cong\Omega^{1}((\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}J_{G})^{m}).$$
###### Proof.
Since the graph
$G$
is closed both the ideal
$J_{G}$
and its initial ideal
$\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G})$
are generated in degree two. If there are no induced
$K_{4}$
graphs, then
$L_{(m,2m)}=0$
and by
3.2
it follows that
$\Omega_{1}(J_{G}^{m})\cong L_{(m,-)}$
. Likewise, if there are no induced
$K_{4}$
graphs, then
$L^{\prime}_{(m,2m)}=0$
and by
3.2
we have
$\Omega_{1}(J_{G}^{m})\cong L_{(m,-)}$
.
∎
Recall the definitions of Eagon-Northcott and Eagon-Northcott type relations from (
2.4
) and (
2.5
), respectively. With an eye towards applying
3.3
, we show that the Eagon-Northcott-type and, separately, the Eagon-Northcott relations form a regular sequence in
$S$
.
###### .
Let
$G$
be a closed graph equipped with an interval labeling. Assume that
$G$
has no cliques of size four. Then
(1)
the set of Eagon-Northcott type relations (
2.4
) form a regular sequence.
(2)
the set of Eagon-Northcott relations (
2.5
) form a regular sequence.
###### Proof.
To prove (1) and (2) it suffices to show that the leading monomials of each set of relations form a regular sequence
[
8
]
. Since the leading monomials are the same by the remarks following
2.1
,
we are able to prove (1) and (2) simultaneously.
The set of leading monomials of the Eagon-Northcott type and Eagon-Northcott (
2.4
) relations with respect to the monomial order discussed in (
2.6
) and the order
$\prec$
described after this equation, respectively,
is
$$\mathcal{L}=\left\{x_{i}T_{jk},y_{j}T_{ik}\mid\{i<j<k\}\text{ is a clique in }G\right\}.$$
We now show that the monomials listed in
$\mathcal{L}$
are pairwise coprime, which is equivalent to them forming a regular sequence.
In our proof we shall employ an alternate characterization of closed graphs from
[
3
, Proposition 4.8]
:
$G$
is closed if and only if for all integers
$1\leq i<j<k\leq$
n, if
$\{i,k\}\in E(G)$
, then
$\{i,j\}\in E(G)$
and
$\{j,k\}\in E(G)$
. We refer to this property as the closed labeling condition.
Consider the following cases:
Case 1:
If
$x_{i}T_{jk}\in\mathcal{L}$
and
$x_{i}^{\prime}T_{j^{\prime}k^{\prime}}\in\mathcal{L}$
are distinct monomials, then
$\{i<j<k\}$
and
$\{i^{\prime}<j^{\prime}<k^{\prime}\}$
are cliques in
$G$
. If
$i=i^{\prime}$
then by the definition of closed graphs
$\{j,j^{\prime}\},\{j,k^{\prime}\},\{j^{\prime},k\},\{k,k^{\prime}\}\in E(G)$
, so
$\{i,j,k,j^{\prime},k^{\prime}\}$
is a clique in
$G$
of size at least four. If
$j=j^{\prime}$
and
$k=k^{\prime}$
then because
$G$
is closed
$\{i,i^{\prime}\}\in E(G)$
, so
$\{i,j,k,i^{\prime}\}$
is a clique in
$G$
of size four. Since both possibilities contradict the hypothesis, we conclude that
$x_{i}T_{jk}$
and
$x_{i}^{\prime}T_{j^{\prime}k^{\prime}}$
are coprime.
Case 2:
If
$x_{i}T_{jk}\in\mathcal{L}$
and
$y_{j^{\prime}}T_{i^{\prime}k^{\prime}}\in\mathcal{L}$
are distinct monomials, then
$\{i<j<k\}$
and
$\{i^{\prime}<j^{\prime}<k^{\prime}\}$
are cliques in
$G$
. If
$j=i^{\prime}$
and
$k=k^{\prime}$
then we have
$i<j=i^{\prime}<j^{\prime}<k=k^{\prime}$
. By the closed labeling condition since
$\{i,k\}$
is an edge of
$G$
,
$\{i,j,j^{\prime},k\}$
is a clique in
$G$
of size four. Since this contradicts the hypothesis, we conclude that
$x_{i}T_{jk}$
and
$y_{j^{\prime}}T_{i^{\prime}k^{\prime}}$
are coprime.
Case 3:
If
$y_{j}T_{ik}\in\mathcal{L}$
and
$y_{j^{\prime}}T_{i^{\prime}k^{\prime}}\in\mathcal{L}$
are distinct monomials, then
$\{i<j<k\}$
and
$\{i^{\prime}<j^{\prime}<k^{\prime}\}$
are cliques in
$G$
. If
$j=j^{\prime}$
then because
$G$
is closed at least one of the pairs
$\{i,i^{\prime}\}$
and
$\{k,k^{\prime}\}$
is distinct and is an edge of
$G$
. If
$i\neq i^{\prime}$
and
$\{i,i^{\prime}\}\in E(G)$
, assuming without loss of generality that
$i<i^{\prime}$
, the closed labeling condition applied to
$i<i^{\prime}<k$
yields that these three vertices form a clique and thus
$\{i,i^{\prime},j,k\}$
is a clique in
$G$
of size four. If
$i=i^{\prime}$
and
$k\neq k^{\prime}$
with
$\{k,k^{\prime}\}\in E(G)$
, then
$\{i,j,k,k^{\prime}\}$
is a clique of size four.
If
$i=i^{\prime}$
and
$k=k^{\prime}$
then we have
$i=i^{\prime}<j,j^{\prime}<k=k^{\prime}$
and by the closed labeling condition it follows that
$\{i,j,j^{\prime},k\}$
is a clique in
$G$
of size four. Since every possibility contradicts the hypothesis, we conclude that
$x_{i}T_{jk}$
and
$y_{j}^{\prime}T_{i^{\prime}k^{\prime}}$
are coprime.
In view of the arguments preceding the case analysis, this concludes the proof.
∎
We can apply this result to the binomial edge ideal of a
$K_{4}$
-free graph to obtian our main result.
###### .
Let
$G$
be a closed
$K_{4}$
-free graph with
$e$
edges and
$t$
triangles (3-cycles). The Betti numbers in the
$2m$
-linear strands of
$J_{G}^{m}$
and
$\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G})^{m}$
are given by
$$\beta_{i,2m+i}(J_{G}^{m})=\beta_{i,2m+i}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G})^{m})=\binom{e+m-i-1}{m-i}\binom{2t}{i}.$$
###### Proof.
Fix an integer
$m\geq 1$
and let
$L$
and
$L^{\prime}$
denote the set of relations of
$\mathcal{R}(J_{G})$
and of
$\mathcal{R}(\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G}))$
, respectively. The assumption that
$G$
is
$K_{4}$
-free ensures that there are no Plücker or Plücker type relations, therefore by
2.1
we have
$L_{(m,2m)}=L^{\prime}_{(m,2m)}=0$
.
By the
Lemma
3.5
, the set of Eagon-Northcott relations and the set of Eagon-Northcott type relations given in
2.1
form a regular sequence. Moreover,
for degree reasons based on
2.1
, every element of
$L_{(m,2m+1)}$

$L^{\prime}_{(m,2m+1)}$

3.3
both for
$J_{G}$
and for
$\operatorname{in}_{\operatorname{lex}}(J_{G})$
with
$r=2t$
(the number of Eagon-Northcott or Eagon-Northcott type relations) and

.
